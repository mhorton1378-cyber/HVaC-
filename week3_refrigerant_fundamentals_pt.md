## Learning Objectives

By the end of this video, a technician will be able to:
1. Explain the refrigerant cycle through its four stages using correct terminology.
2. Use a pressure-temperature (P/T) chart to find the saturation temperature for a given refrigerant and pressure.
3. Identify the two most common refrigerants in multifamily systems and state the key safety differences between them.

## SEGMENT 2 – The Refrigerant Cycle: Four Stages
**Time Range: 0:45 – 2:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor next to an animated refrigerant cycle diagram on a display or poster.)*

"The refrigerant circuit does one job: it moves heat from inside the conditioned space to outside. That's it. It's not making cold — it's moving heat. The indoor space feels cold because heat has been removed from it.

Here's how it happens in four stages.

**Stage one — Compression.** Low-pressure, low-temperature refrigerant vapor enters the compressor. The compressor squeezes it, and as any gas gets compressed, its temperature rises dramatically. The refrigerant leaves the compressor as a high-pressure, high-temperature vapor. This is the high side of the system.

**Stage two — Condensation.** That hot vapor enters the condenser coil — the outdoor unit. The condenser fan blows outdoor air across the coil. The refrigerant releases its heat to the outdoor air and changes state: vapor condenses into a liquid. It's still at high pressure, but now it's a warm liquid.

**Stage three — Metering.** The high-pressure liquid hits a metering device — either a fixed orifice or a thermostatic expansion valve, a TXV. This device restricts the flow, causing a sudden pressure drop. When pressure drops, temperature drops with it. We go from warm high-pressure liquid to cold low-pressure liquid. This is the transition to the low side.

**Stage four — Evaporation.** The cold low-pressure liquid enters the evaporator coil — the indoor coil. Warm air from the space blows across the coil. The refrigerant absorbs that heat and changes state again: the liquid boils into a vapor. That vapor then travels back to the compressor to repeat the cycle.

Two sides, two pressures, two temperatures. High side: hot and high pressure. Low side: cold and low pressure. Once you see the system this way, everything else makes sense."

## SEGMENT 3 – The Pressure-Temperature Relationship
**Time Range: 2:30 – 4:15**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor holding a laminated P/T chart.)*

"Here's the most important concept in refrigerant diagnosis: at a given pressure, a pure refrigerant will always boil or condense at a specific temperature. Always. This is not variable — it's physics.

We call this the saturation temperature. And we use a pressure-temperature chart to find it.

*(Opens P/T chart.)*

This chart has pressure on one axis and temperature on the other, for each refrigerant type. Let's use R-410A — the refrigerant in most modern split systems in multifamily.

If I'm reading 115 PSI on the low side of an R-410A system, I look that up on the chart. That corresponds to a saturation temperature of about 40 degrees Fahrenheit. That's the temperature at which the refrigerant is boiling inside the evaporator coil at that pressure.

Now here's why that matters. If the actual temperature of the suction line coming out of the evaporator is higher than the saturation temperature — say it's 50 degrees instead of 40 — the refrigerant has fully boiled and picked up extra heat. That extra heat above saturation is called **superheat**, and we'll cover how to use it in Week 4.

Right now, the key habit to build is: **look up your pressure on the P/T chart every time.** Don't guess. Don't memorize numbers. The chart is your reference because every refrigerant has different P/T curves.

Speaking of which — you need to know which refrigerant you're working with before you touch the gauges."

## SEGMENT 4 – Refrigerant Types: R-22 and R-410A
**Time Range: 4:15 – 5:45**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor holding two refrigerant cylinders.)*

"In multifamily you're going to encounter primarily two refrigerants on existing equipment.

**R-22** — also called Freon, though that's actually a brand name — is the white cylinder. R-22 has been phased out of new equipment since 2010, and as of 2020 it can no longer be manufactured or imported in the U.S. You can still buy reclaimed R-22, but it's expensive. Any system still running R-22 is at minimum 15 years old. If it has a significant leak, a retrofit or replacement conversation needs to happen.

**R-410A** — the pink cylinder — is the current standard in most split systems installed in the last decade. It operates at significantly higher pressures than R-22 — roughly double — so your gauges, hoses, and equipment must be rated for R-410A. You cannot use R-22 gauges on R-410A systems.

How do you know which refrigerant is in the system? Check the nameplate on the outdoor unit. It's printed there. Check before you connect anything.

One critical safety note: **never mix refrigerants.** Never add R-410A to an R-22 system or vice versa. Mixing creates an unspecified blend with unknown P/T characteristics and can damage compressors and void warranties. If a system's refrigerant type is unknown, treat it as unknown and investigate before adding anything.

We're not going to connect gauges yet in this series — that requires EPA Section 608 certification, and we'll talk about that in Week 8. But you need to understand P/T before gauges mean anything, which is why we cover it here."

## SEGMENT 5 – Reading a Scenario: Putting P/T Into Practice
**Time Range: 5:45 – 7:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at a whiteboard or on-screen scenario graphic.)*

"Let me give you a real scenario so you can see how P/T thinking changes your diagnosis — even before you pick up a gauge.

Scenario: A system has been running all day, the resident says it's just not cooling enough. You do your Step 1 through 3 from Week 1 — thermostat is fine, power is good, contactor is pulled in, everything is running. But the air coming out of the supply is only 10 degrees cooler than the return air, and it should be 15 to 20.

Now you have a choice: is this a refrigerant issue or an airflow issue?

Before you assume refrigerant, think through airflow. Pull the filter — is it dirty? Check the coil — is it iced or restricted? If airflow is compromised, the evaporator can't absorb heat efficiently, the refrigerant can't pick up enough heat, and your temperature drop shrinks.

If airflow is solid, then you suspect the refrigerant circuit. That's when P/T and gauges become your tool.

A low refrigerant charge means low suction pressure. Low suction pressure means the saturation temperature inside the evaporator is unusually cold — maybe 20°F instead of 40°F — which causes the coil to ice over. So an iced coil doesn't just mean dirty filter. It also means look at refrigerant.

The point: **airflow first, always.** Refrigerant diagnosis with gauges confirms what your eyes and hands already suspect. It doesn't replace the visual checks."

## SEGMENT 6 – P/T Chart Practice & Field Assignment
**Time Range: 7:30 – 9:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at desk with P/T chart and a short worksheet visible.)*

"Here is the single most effective exercise you can do this week to build P/T fluency: drill the chart.

I want you to take a P/T chart — there's a printable version in the course materials — and run through these five lookups for R-410A:

What is the saturation temperature at 100 PSI? At 130 PSI? At 200 PSI? At 70 PSI? And at 250 PSI?

Do those lookups, write down the temperatures, and do it again tomorrow. In three days you'll have a feel for the normal operating range without even thinking about it.

The second part of your assignment: on your next three service calls — even PMs, even no-cool calls where the issue is something else — look at the nameplate on the outdoor unit and write down the refrigerant type. Get comfortable finding that information quickly.

Knowing your P/T chart and knowing your refrigerant are two habits that cost you nothing and make every future diagnosis faster."
