## SEGMENT 2 – Step 1 (Heat Mode): Thermostat & Initial Checks
**Time Range: 0:50 – 2:15**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at thermostat.)*

"Step one is the same starting point as a no-cool call — the thermostat. But now we're checking for heat.

Mode: set to Heat, not Cool, not Emergency Heat. Setpoint: at least 3 to 5 degrees above current room temperature.

When you set the call, you're listening for the blower to start — but on a furnace system, there's a critical difference from cooling: **the blower doesn't start immediately.** On a gas furnace, the sequence is: call for heat → ignition → burners fire → heat exchanger warms up → then the blower starts — typically 30 to 60 seconds after ignition. This is by design. Running a cold blower over a cold heat exchanger would just blow cold air.

If you make the call and hear nothing at all — no click, no attempt at ignition — that's a power or control issue. Go to Step 2.

If you hear the furnace trying to ignite — clicking, a brief flame, then shutting off — that's a lockout. Let the furnace complete its ignition sequence. Most furnaces make three to five ignition attempts before locking out and flashing a fault code. Count the blinks.

Feel the supply register after 90 seconds. Warm air? System is working. No air, or cool air with the blower running? Go to Step 3."

## SEGMENT 3 – Step 2 & 3 (Gas Furnace): Safety First, Then Diagnosis
**Time Range: 2:15 – 4:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at gas furnace — serious, deliberate tone.)*

"On a gas furnace, before you open anything, three safety checks. Every time. Non-negotiable.

**One — smell for gas.** If you detect a gas odor at or near the furnace, do not proceed with electrical diagnosis. Evacuate, ventilate, and call your gas provider. This is not an HVAC diagnosis situation.

**Two — verify the CO detector is functional.** Carbon monoxide from a cracked heat exchanger is invisible and odorless. If a property doesn't have CO detectors in units with gas appliances, that's a safety issue to flag immediately.

**Three — check the flue and combustion air.** Make sure the furnace exhaust flue is intact, not blocked by birds' nests or debris, and properly connected.

With those three done, open the furnace and look at the fault code LED on the control board. Most modern furnaces have a window or sight glass so you can watch the burner attempt.

*(Looks through sight glass.)*

Watch the ignition sequence. You should see the hot surface ignitor glow — it looks like a small orange element — then the gas valve opens and the burners light. If the ignitor glows but the burners don't light, suspect the gas valve or a flame sensor issue. If the ignitor doesn't glow, suspect the ignitor or a control board output.

The flame sensor is a single metal rod that sits in the flame. It proves to the board that the burner actually lit. A dirty flame sensor — common after a summer of sitting idle — doesn't conduct the microamp current the board is looking for, so the board cuts the gas as a safety measure.

Cleaning the flame sensor is one of the most common and simple gas furnace repairs you'll make: disconnect the wire, pull the sensor, and clean the rod lightly with fine steel wool or emery cloth. Reconnect and test."

## SEGMENT 4 – Heat Pump Basics: The Reversing Valve
**Time Range: 4:30 – 6:15**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at outdoor heat pump unit.)*

"Now let's talk about heat pumps, because multifamily properties are increasingly using them — and they have some behaviors that will trip you up if you don't understand how they work.

A heat pump is a refrigeration system that can run in two directions. In cooling mode, it works exactly like a split system — moving heat from inside to outside. In heating mode, it reverses: it extracts heat from outdoor air — even cold outdoor air — and brings it inside.

The component that makes this possible is the **reversing valve**. It's a solenoid-controlled valve in the refrigerant circuit that redirects refrigerant flow between heating and cooling modes.

*(Shows reversing valve on outdoor unit or diagram.)*

The reversing valve is typically energized in cooling and de-energized in heating — though some manufacturers do the opposite. Check the wiring diagram. If the reversing valve is stuck or failed, the system may run in the wrong mode — cooling when it should be heating, or vice versa.

Testing the reversing valve: with the thermostat switching between modes, you should be able to measure 24 volts AC at the reversing valve solenoid coil terminals in the energized mode, and 0 volts in the de-energized mode. If voltage is correct but the valve isn't shifting — you can sometimes hear a click when it shifts — the valve itself may be stuck. A stuck reversing valve requires a refrigerant recovery and valve replacement by a certified technician.

One more behavior to know: heat pumps in heating mode will periodically go into a **defrost cycle**. Ice builds up on the outdoor coil when extracting heat from cold outdoor air. The defrost board switches the system temporarily back to cooling mode to melt the ice — the outdoor unit steams, the indoor blower may stop or switch to auxiliary heat, and the outdoor fan stops. This is completely normal. It lasts 5 to 15 minutes.

A resident calling about the outdoor unit steaming and air stopping is likely watching a defrost cycle. Know this before you go out."

## SEGMENT 5 – Electric Heat & Auxiliary/Emergency Heat
**Time Range: 6:15 – 7:45**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at air handler with electric heat strips visible.)*

"Heat pumps become less efficient as outdoor temperature drops. Below about 35 to 40 degrees Fahrenheit, the system can't extract enough heat from outdoor air to meet the load alone. That's where **auxiliary heat** kicks in — typically electric resistance heat strips inside the air handler.

Auxiliary heat is controlled automatically by the thermostat based on temperature differential. When the space is more than 2 to 3 degrees below setpoint, the aux heat stages on to help. On the thermostat, you'll see 'AUX' or 'AUX HEAT' displayed — this is normal operation.

**Emergency heat** is different. When the thermostat is set to emergency heat mode, it completely bypasses the heat pump compressor and runs only the electric heat strips. This is used when the heat pump fails — it's a backup mode, not a normal operating mode. Running a system on emergency heat exclusively is expensive and should only be a temporary measure while the heat pump is repaired.

For a no-heat call on an all-electric system with heat strips:

Step 1: Thermostat check — same as always.
Step 2: Verify power at the air handler. Electric heat strips run on line voltage, typically 240V. They also have sequencers — time-delay relays that stage the strips on one at a time to prevent a large inrush current. A failed sequencer means one or more heat stages won't energize. Test for line voltage at each strip while the thermostat is calling for heat.
Step 3: Check the breaker. Electric heat strips draw significant amperage. A tripped breaker specifically for the heat strips — sometimes a separate breaker from the main air handler breaker — is a common find."

---

## SEGMENT 6 – Documentation & Field Assignment
**Time Range: 7:45 – 9:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at workbench with work order and clipboard.)*

"No-heat calls have one additional documentation requirement that no-cool calls don't: you need to document whether the space was safe when you arrived and when you left.

If you found the temperature below 60 degrees in a unit with a resident — especially with vulnerable populations like elderly residents or families with young children — note the temperature, note what you did to restore heat, and note if temporary heat was provided. Many states and local housing codes have specific requirements about habitability temperature. Your manager and your property need a record.

Work order example: 'No heat call. Arrived — indoor temp 54°F. Furnace in lockout, LED flashing 4 blinks (code 4 — dirty flame sensor). Cleaned flame sensor, reset furnace. Furnace ignited and ran through full cycle. Verified warm air at supply — 110°F discharge. Indoor temp 62°F at departure. Resident informed system is operational. Bucket: Thermostat/Control (flame sensor).'

Your field assignment this week: locate the furnace or heat strips in three different units in your property. Identify the heat source type — gas furnace, electric heat strips, heat pump, or combination. Note where the safety switches are — limit switch, flame sensor, rollout switch. You're building your mental map before you need it on an emergency call."
