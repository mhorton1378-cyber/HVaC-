## SEGMENT 2 – Superheat: What It Is and What It Tells You
**Time Range: 0:50 – 2:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at refrigerant cycle diagram, pointing to evaporator outlet.)*

"Superheat is the temperature rise of the refrigerant vapor above its boiling point at a given pressure. It's measured at the **suction line** — the large insulated line coming out of the evaporator coil.

Here's how you calculate it.

Step one: Read the low-side pressure on your manifold gauge. Let's say it's 115 PSI on an R-410A system.

Step two: Look that pressure up on your P/T chart. 115 PSI on R-410A corresponds to about 40°F saturation temperature.

Step three: Clamp a temperature probe onto the suction line — the large insulated line at the outdoor unit. Let's say it reads 55°F.

Step four: Subtract. 55°F minus 40°F equals 15°F of superheat.

What does that number mean?

On a fixed-orifice system — most residential split systems — the target evaporator superheat is typically in the range of 10 to 25 degrees. The exact target depends on indoor wet-bulb temperature and outdoor temperature; manufacturers publish charging charts for this.

**Low superheat** — below about 5 degrees — means the evaporator is flooding with liquid refrigerant that isn't fully boiling. Liquid refrigerant reaching the compressor is called slugging, and it can damage the compressor immediately.

**High superheat** — above 25 to 30 degrees — typically means low refrigerant charge, or a restriction in the metering device. The refrigerant is boiling off too fast and has fully evaporated before the end of the coil. The system isn't absorbing as much heat as it should.

Superheat is your primary indicator of the low side. But for the high side, we use subcooling."

## SEGMENT 3 – Subcooling: What It Is and What It Tells You
**Time Range: 2:30 – 4:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor pointing to liquid line exiting the condenser.)*

"Subcooling works the same way mathematically, but on the high side.

Step one: Read the high-side pressure on your manifold gauge. Let's say it's 370 PSI on R-410A.

Step two: Look up 370 PSI on the R-410A P/T chart. That corresponds to about 105°F saturation temperature — the temperature at which the refrigerant condenses from vapor to liquid in the condenser.

Step three: Clamp your temperature probe on the liquid line — the small line leaving the condenser. Let's say it reads 90°F.

Step four: Subtract. 105°F minus 90°F equals 15°F of subcooling.

What does that number mean?

Target subcooling for most TXV systems is 10 to 15 degrees. For fixed-orifice systems it's similar. Always check the manufacturer's spec on the nameplate or service data.

**Low subcooling** — below 5 degrees — means the condenser doesn't have enough liquid refrigerant to fully condense. Vapor is reaching the metering device. That means low charge, or a refrigerant leak.

**High subcooling** — above 20 degrees — usually points to refrigerant overcharge. There's too much liquid backed up in the condenser. Overcharge raises head pressure, makes the compressor work harder, and shortens its life.

Together, superheat and subcooling tell a complete story:

- Both in range → charge is correct.
- Low superheat + high subcooling → overcharge or a metering device restriction.
- High superheat + low subcooling → undercharge, likely a leak.
- High superheat + normal subcooling → metering device restriction or airflow issue.

These combinations are your diagnosis, not just your readings."

---

### Visual / Camera Notes:

- **Camera framing:** MCU at diagram pointing to liquid line. ECU of temperature clamp on liquid line.
- **UI overlays:**
  - Subcooling formula: *"Saturation Temp (from P/T chart) − Liquid Line Temp = Subcooling"*
  - Worked example: 105°F − 90°F = 15°F SC — highlighted green.
  - 2×2 diagnosis matrix:
    - Low SH + High SC → Overcharge/TXV restriction
    - High SH + Low SC → Undercharge/Leak
    - High SH + Normal SC → Metering/Airflow

## SEGMENT 4 – When NOT to Charge: The Non-Negotiables
**Time Range: 4:00 – 5:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor faces camera directly — serious tone.)*

"Before we talk about adding refrigerant, we need to talk about when you do NOT add refrigerant. This matters.

**Do not add refrigerant when there is an active leak.** Adding refrigerant to a leaking system is not a repair. It's a temporary fix that delays the actual repair, costs money, and releases refrigerant to the atmosphere — which is an EPA violation. Find the leak first. Repair or document it. Then charge.

**Do not add refrigerant when airflow is restricted.** We said this in Week 3 and we'll keep saying it. A clogged filter or dirty coil creates low suction pressure that looks exactly like low refrigerant. Add refrigerant to a restricted system and you'll likely overcharge it once the airflow issue is resolved.

**Do not add refrigerant without gauges and a P/T chart.** Adding refrigerant by feel, by sound, or by sight glass alone is not a diagnostic approach. Connect gauges, calculate superheat and subcooling, compare to manufacturer spec, then make a decision.

**Do not add refrigerant if you are not EPA Section 608 certified.** Purchasing and handling refrigerant without certification is a federal violation. If you're not yet certified, your job right now is to assist and observe a certified technician — and sign up for your 608 exam. We talk about that in Week 8.

The point of all this: refrigerant diagnosis is a precision task. These rules protect the equipment, protect you, and protect your property from liability."

## SEGMENT 5 – Walking Through a Live Gauge Reading
**Time Range: 5:30 – 7:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor with manifold gauges connected to an outdoor unit — system running.)*

"Let's walk through a real gauge reading so you see how superheat and subcooling work together in the field. I'm going to narrate exactly what I'd do on a call.

System is running. I've confirmed the call is for low cooling — not an electrical issue, airflow checks out, filter is clean. I'm going to connect my gauge set.

*(Connects low-side hose to suction service port, high-side to liquid service port.)*

Before I connect, note: I purge my hoses to remove air. Air in the refrigerant circuit causes inaccurate readings and can contaminate the system.

Low side: reading 100 PSI. High side: reading 385 PSI.

Outdoor ambient today is 92°F. Indoor return air wet bulb is 67°F.

Low side first. R-410A, 100 PSI — looking up the P/T chart — that gives me a saturation temp of about 36°F. Now I clamp my probe on the suction line right at the service port.

*(Clamps probe — reads 58°F.)*

58 minus 36 is 22 degrees of superheat. Manufacturer's charging chart for this unit at these conditions calls for 14 degrees. I'm reading 22. Superheat is high.

High side. 385 PSI on R-410A corresponds to about 110°F saturation. I clamp the liquid line.

*(Liquid line temp reads 98°F.)*

110 minus 98 equals 12 degrees subcooling. Manufacturer targets 10 to 14 degrees SC. I'm right in range.

High superheat plus normal subcooling — look at the matrix. That combination points to a metering device restriction or an airflow issue. I've already cleared airflow, so I'm going to inspect the TXV before I make any refrigerant decision.

See how that works? The gauges pointed me to the right component — without guessing."

## SEGMENT 6 – Documenting Gauge Readings & Field Assignment
**Time Range: 7:30 – 9:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at workbench with work order clipboard.)*

"Every time you connect gauges, document your readings. This is the work order note that actually tells a story.

Here's the standard: record low-side pressure, high-side pressure, suction line temperature, liquid line temperature, calculated superheat, calculated subcooling, outdoor ambient temp, and indoor wet-bulb or return air temperature. Then note your diagnosis.

Example: 'R-410A system. Low side 100 PSI, high side 385 PSI. Suction line 58°F, liquid line 98°F. SH 22°F (target 14°F) — high. SC 12°F — normal. Airflow confirmed clean. Suspect TXV restriction. Recommend TXV inspection/replacement. Refrigerant not added.'

That note is comprehensive, accurate, and tells anyone picking up this work order exactly what was found and why no refrigerant was added.

Your field assignment this week: find a unit with a certified technician and observe a complete gauge reading. Watch them calculate superheat and subcooling. Write down the numbers yourself. After the call, check their math with a P/T chart.

If you're not yet EPA 608 certified, this week is also a great time to schedule your exam. We put resources in the course materials."

**Q1 — Multiple Choice**

- A) 12°F ✓
- B) 52°F
- C) 40°F
- D) 92°F

**Q2 — Multiple Choice**
*A system shows high superheat and normal subcooling. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?*
- A) Refrigerant overcharge
- B) Low superheat / compressor flooding
- C) Metering device restriction or airflow issue ✓
- D) Condenser fan motor failure

**Q3 — Multiple Choice**
*Why should you NOT add refrigerant to a system that has a known leak?*
- A) Refrigerant is too expensive to waste on a leak.
- B) Adding refrigerant to a leaking system delays the actual repair, wastes refrigerant, and may violate EPA regulations. ✓
- C) Refrigerant leaks always fix themselves over time.
- D) A leaking system will not accept refrigerant.

**Q4 — Multiple Choice**
*What does low subcooling (below 5°F) at the liquid line most likely indicate?*
- A) The system is overcharged.
- B) The condenser coil is dirty.
- C) The refrigerant charge is low — vapor may be reaching the metering device. ✓
- D) The suction line temperature is too high.

**Q5 — Short Answer**
*You are observing a gauge reading. Low side reads 70 PSI (R-410A saturation = 26°F); suction line temperature = 55°F. Calculate superheat. Is this reading within a normal range (10–25°F)?*
*(Expected: 55 − 26 = 29°F superheat. This is above the typical 10–25°F range — high superheat, indicates possible low charge or metering restriction.)*
