## SEGMENT 2 – The PM Checklist: Your Field Standard
**Time Range: 0:50 – 2:15**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at clipboard with printed PM checklist.)*

"A PM has four categories: outdoor unit, indoor unit, electrical, and documentation. Let's break down each one.

**Outdoor unit:**
- Clear a minimum 18-inch perimeter of debris, vegetation, and storage.
- Inspect and clean condenser coil fins.
- Inspect condenser fan blade and motor mounts — look for cracks, bent blades, loose hardware.
- Straighten bent coil fins with a fin comb.
- Verify disconnect is properly rated and fuses are intact.
- Inspect refrigerant lines and insulation — look for damaged line set insulation, oil staining at fittings (sign of a refrigerant leak), or deteriorated pipe insulation on the suction line.

**Indoor unit:**
- Replace air filter — note MERV rating and use specified or approved equivalent.
- Inspect evaporator coil — clean if dirty, check for ice or moisture issues.
- Clear condensate drain — flush with water, check for algae, verify proper trap and slope.
- Inspect blower wheel — look for dirt buildup, which reduces airflow dramatically.
- Check blower motor mounts and belt tension if applicable.

**Electrical:**
- Test capacitor µF on both FAN and HERM sides.
- Inspect contactor contacts for pitting or burning.
- Torque all electrical terminal connections — vibration loosens them over time.
- Verify transformer output voltage.
- Measure and record operating amperage on compressor and fan motors — compare to nameplate.

**Documentation:**
- Record all findings including measurements.
- Note any deferred items with a recommended follow-up date.
- Photograph any concerning conditions — coil damage, burned contacts, deteriorated insulation.

That's your standard. Every item, every visit."

## SEGMENT 3 – Coil Cleaning: Condenser (Outdoor)
**Time Range: 2:15 – 4:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at condenser unit with coil cleaner, garden hose, and safety glasses.)*

"Cleaning the condenser coil is one of the highest-impact maintenance tasks you do. A coil that's 15% fouled reduces system efficiency by 15 to 20%, raises head pressure, and puts the compressor under stress. Over a summer, that's real money in energy costs and compressor wear.

Here's the correct technique.

First — pull the disconnect and lock it out. You're going to be spraying water near electrical components.

Apply a non-acid coil cleaner — follow the label directions, typically foam it on and let it dwell for 5 to 10 minutes. Coil cleaner is formulated to break down the oils and fine debris that pack into the fins.

*(Applies foam coil cleaner.)*

While it dwells, inspect the fins. Use a fin comb to straighten any sections of bent fins. A few minutes with a fin comb can meaningfully improve airflow.

Now rinse — and here's the key technique: **rinse from inside out.** Put the hose nozzle inside the unit, spray outward through the fins. This pushes debris out the way it came in, rather than compacting it deeper into the coil. Do not use a pressure washer — the fan fins are thin aluminum and will bend under high pressure. A standard garden hose with a spray nozzle is correct.

*(Rinses from inside out — debris washes out.)*

Rinse until the water runs clear. Let the coil drain, then restore the disconnect and test the system."

## SEGMENT 4 – Coil Cleaning: Evaporator (Indoor) & Condensate Drain
**Time Range: 4:00 – 5:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at open air handler — evaporator coil visible.)*

"Indoor coil cleaning is different in a few ways. You can't spray water freely inside an air handler without making a mess and potentially damaging the control board and electrical components.

First, inspect the coil. Shine a flashlight through the coil face. You're looking for:

- Dirt embedded in the fins — reduces airflow and heat transfer.
- Ice — if the coil is iced, stop and defrost before any cleaning.
- Microbial growth — dark streaks or patches. Common in humid climates and often visible at the coil face or drain pan.

For light contamination on the indoor coil, use a **no-rinse evaporator coil cleaner** — it's a foam that breaks down deposits and drains into the drain pan. Apply, let it dwell, and it drains away.

For heavy fouling, the coil may need to come out for proper cleaning — that's a more significant maintenance event.

Now the condensate drain. This is the one PM item that, when skipped, causes more water damage in multifamily than almost anything else. Algae and slime grow in the drain pan and condensate line, block the drain, and water overflows into drywall, flooring, or ceiling below.

Monthly or quarterly, depending on your climate:

- Check the drain pan for standing water — there should be none.
- Flush the drain line with a cup of water and confirm it flows freely at the termination point.
- Add a condensate treatment tablet to the pan — they inhibit algae growth.
- If the line is blocked, clear it with a wet/dry vac on the termination end, or a flush with diluted bleach solution.

A five-minute condensate check prevents a $3,000 water damage claim. Do not skip it."

## SEGMENT 5 – Electrical PM: Capacitors, Contacts & Connections
**Time Range: 5:30 – 7:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at open condenser panel — capacitor and contactor visible.)*

"The electrical portion of a PM takes 10 to 15 minutes and catches the two most common no-cool causes before they happen.

**Capacitors:** Test every capacitor on every PM. Record the rated µF and your measured µF. A capacitor that tests at 85% of its rated value is weak — it hasn't failed yet, but it's trending toward failure. Proactive replacement during a scheduled PM costs a fraction of an emergency call at 3 PM on a 95-degree Saturday.

*(Tests capacitor — reads 38.9 µF against 45 µF rated.)*

That's 86% — I'm going to note it, discuss replacement with my supervisor, and schedule it before the next heat wave.

**Contactors:** Pull the disconnect, inspect the contactor contacts. They should be smooth and silver. If they're pitted, black, or have a burned appearance — replace the contactor. Pitted contacts cause voltage drop across the contactor, which causes the compressor to run on reduced voltage. Running a compressor on reduced voltage causes overheating and early failure.

*(Shows pitted vs. clean contactor contacts.)*

**Electrical connections:** With power off, use an insulated screwdriver or a torque wrench to check that every electrical terminal is snug. Vibration from normal operation loosens connections over time. A loose connection creates resistance, resistance creates heat, and heat eventually causes a failure or a fire. Two minutes on terminal torque, every PM.

**Operating amperage:** Once the system is running, clamp your ammeter around the compressor wire. Record the amperage and compare to the nameplate RLA — rated load amps. Running near or above RLA is a sign the compressor is working harder than it should. Document it."

## SEGMENT 6 – PM Documentation & the "Deferred Item" Log
**Time Range: 7:00 – 8:15**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at workbench with PM form and work order.)*

"A PM is only as good as its documentation. Here's the professional standard.

Every item on the checklist gets a note: pass, fail, or defer. Pass is fine. Fail means you repaired it today. Defer means you found a condition that needs action but couldn't complete it on this visit — and it needs a follow-up work order immediately.

Do not leave a deferred item undocumented. Create the follow-up work order before you leave that unit.

Your measurements — capacitor µF readings, operating amperage, transformer voltage — go on the form every time. Over time, those numbers tell a story. A capacitor that reads 44 µF in April, 42 µF in October, and 39 µF the following April is trending downward. You can replace it before it causes a call.

This is the difference between reactive maintenance and proactive maintenance. Proactive maintenance requires data over time. Data over time requires consistent documentation.

Sample documentation entry: 'Unit 214 – Spring PM. Condenser coil cleaned — moderate fouling. Filter replaced — MERV-8. Capacitor tested: FAN 4.9 µF (5.0 rated, OK), HERM 38.9 µF (45 rated, 86% — flagged for proactive replacement). Contactor contacts inspected — good. Condensate drain flushed — clear. Compressor amperage 12.4A (13.5A RLA — normal). Deferred: proactive capacitor replacement — WO #5471 created.'"
