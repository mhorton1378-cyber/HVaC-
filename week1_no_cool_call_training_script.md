## Learning Objectives

By the end of this video, a technician will be able to:
1. Follow a consistent four-step diagnostic pattern on any no-cool call.
2. Use a meter safely to verify 24V control voltage and line voltage at the air handler and condenser.
3. Categorize the root cause into one of four diagnostic buckets and document clear next steps.

## SEGMENT 2 – The Four-Step Diagnostic Pattern (Framework Intro)
**Time Range: 0:45 – 2:00** *(approx. 175 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor steps closer to camera, gestures naturally.)*

"Every no-cool call we run on a split system — an indoor air handler paired with an outdoor condenser — follows the same four-step pattern. I call them the four buckets, and by the end of this video you're going to own them.

Step one: **Thermostat and initial checks.** We start at the source of the call — the thermostat. Before we touch any equipment, we figure out whether the system was even asked to cool.

Step two: **Indoor unit — power and airflow.** We head to the air handler, verify we have power, verify we have control signal, and we look at the filter and coil.

Step three: **Outdoor unit — call and components.** We go outside, confirm the condenser is getting the call, and we test the electrical components that run the show.

Step four: **Bucket the problem and document.** We name what we found, put it in the right bucket, and write a work order that tells the whole story.

These four steps — in order — every time. Let's dig in."

---

### Visual / Camera Notes:

- **Camera framing:** Medium shot, instructor standing next to a wall-mounted 4-bucket diagram (printed or digital display behind them).
- **UI overlay — 4-Bucket Diagram:** A clean graphic appears on screen, showing four labeled boxes arranged in a 2×2 grid:

## SEGMENT 3 – Step 1: Thermostat & Initial Checks
**Time Range: 2:00 – 3:45** *(approx. 245 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor faces the thermostat on the wall — a standard programmable or smart thermostat.)*

"Step one starts right here. A lot of techs walk past the thermostat to get to the 'real' equipment. Don't do that. The thermostat is the brain of this call, and it only takes 60 seconds to rule it out — or find your problem right here.

Three things to check:

First — **mode**. Make sure it's set to Cool, not Heat, not just Fan. You'd be surprised.

*(Taps mode button, shows Cool selected.)*

Second — **setpoint**. The setpoint needs to be at least five degrees below the current room temperature before the system is going to call for cooling. If the room is 78 and the setpoint is 77, the system is barely going to twitch.

*(Points to temperature display.)*

Third — **fan setting**. Auto or On. On Auto, the blower only runs when the system is actively conditioning air. On On, it runs constantly. Either can be right depending on the resident's preference, but you need to know which you're dealing with.

Now — while you're standing here, listen. Did you hear a click when you set the call? That click is the thermostat's internal relay closing, sending a signal to the system.

After the click, wait 30 to 60 seconds. Do you feel airflow at the nearest supply vent?

*(Holds hand near supply vent.)*

If you feel cool or warm air — the indoor blower is running. If you feel nothing — the blower may not be running at all.

That single observation tells us which direction we go next."

---

- **Camera framing:** MCU of instructor at thermostat; over-the-shoulder (OTS) closeup shots of thermostat screen as settings are adjusted.
- **UI overlays:**

## SEGMENT 4 – Step 2: Indoor Unit — Power & Control Signal
**Time Range: 3:45 – 5:30** *(approx. 255 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor opens mechanical closet door, air handler visible — a standard vertical or horizontal unit.)*

"Step two. We're at the air handler. The first thing I want you to identify before you do anything else is the service disconnect — usually a small switch in a metal box right on the unit, or a breaker in a sub-panel nearby. This is what you use if you ever need to cut power to this unit. Know where it is before you start.

*(Points to disconnect switch.)*

Now — **do not open the unit panel yet**. First, let's verify we have power at all. You're looking for signs the unit is energized: control board indicator lights, any display, a blower running.

If nothing is running and the thermostat looked right in Step 1, get your meter.

*(Holds up digital multimeter.)*

Set it to AC voltage. We want to find the low-voltage control board terminals — typically a terminal strip labeled R, C, Y, G, and W. Here's what each one means in a cooling call:

- **R** is 24-volt power — hot side.
- **C** is common — the return path.
- **Y** is the cooling call signal — this wire goes out to the contactor on the condenser.
- **G** is the fan call.

Measure R to C first. You want to see 24 volts AC — anywhere between 22 and 28 is normal. No voltage here? You've got either a blown fuse, a tripped breaker, or a failed transformer.

*(Meter probes on R and C — reading appears.)*

Now check Y to C. If the thermostat is calling for cool, that wire should be hot — 24 volts. If R–C is good but Y–C is zero, your control board or thermostat isn't completing the cooling call.

Check G to C as well — that's your blower call.

This is where you can bucket a lot of problems without ever going outside."

---

### Visual / Camera Notes:

- **Camera framing:** Wide shot of instructor opening mechanical closet; MCU of hands pointing to disconnect; extreme closeup (ECU) of multimeter probes at terminal strip.
- **UI overlays:**
  - Terminal strip diagram — labeled R, C, Y, G, W with color-coded wires (red, black/blue, yellow, green, white). Voltage values animate in as instructor names them.
  - Animated voltage icon (lightning bolt) next to "24V AC" reading.
  - Two-column callout box: "R–C = Power present? YES / NO" / "Y–C = Cooling call present? YES / NO."
- **B-roll:** ECU of multimeter display reading 24.3V; close-up of indicator light on control board glowing; hand locating the fuse holder on the board.
- **Safety callout (inline):** Instructor says "set it to AC voltage" — overlay text: *"Always verify meter is set to AC, not DC, before probing live terminals."* Small caution icon.
- **Transition:** Dissolve to instructor now looking at filter rack adjacent to air handler.

## SEGMENT 5 – Step 2 (Continued): Airflow — Filter & Coil
**Time Range: 5:30 – 6:45** *(approx. 180 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor slides out the air filter.)*

"Before we go outside, there's one more thing to check on the indoor unit — and I can't stress this enough. **Airflow is the bloodstream of this system.** If airflow is restricted, the system cannot transfer heat efficiently, the coil will freeze over, and the compressor can fail trying to compensate.

Pull the filter.

*(Holds filter up toward camera.)*

This is what a filter that needs to be replaced looks like. Gray, compacted, barely any light coming through. A filter like this will strangle your system.

Now look at the evaporator coil — it's behind the filter rack. You're looking for:

One — ice. If the coil is frozen, stop the call, switch the thermostat to Fan Only to defrost, and come back. Frozen coil is almost always airflow or refrigerant, and you can't diagnose refrigerant through a block of ice.

Two — dirt and debris packed into the coil face.

Here's the rule: **always fix airflow issues before chasing any refrigerant diagnosis.** Replace the filter, schedule a coil cleaning if needed, and re-run the call after defrost. Don't guess at refrigerant while airflow is compromised."

---

### Visual / Camera Notes:

- **Camera framing:** MCU of instructor pulling filter; ECU of filter held up to light showing blockage vs. a clean filter side-by-side.
- **B-roll:** Clean filter vs. clogged filter held up to a shop light — dramatic contrast. ECU of evaporator coil face (ice on coil vs. clean coil).
- **UI overlays:**
  - Side-by-side comparison graphic: "CLEAN FILTER ✓ / CLOGGED FILTER ✗."
  - Rule card overlay: *"Fix airflow FIRST. Never diagnose refrigerant through a frozen coil."*
- **Animated diagram:** Simple airflow path arrows through the coil — arrows become red and blocked as filter darkens, demonstrating restriction visually.
- **Transition:** Cut to exterior of building — instructor walking around to outdoor condenser.

## SEGMENT 6 – Step 3: Outdoor Unit — Visual Inspection & Power Check
**Time Range: 6:45 – 8:15** *(approx. 215 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor approaches outdoor condenser unit.)*

"Step three — outdoor unit. Start with your eyes before you touch anything.

Walk a circle around it. Look for:

Debris packed into the coil fins. Grass clippings, cottonwood, leaves — these restrict airflow just like a dirty filter, but on the hot side of the system.

Bent or damaged fins. If the fins are heavily matted, that condenser is working harder than it should.

Clearances. There should be at least 18 to 24 inches of clear space around the unit. Overgrown shrubs, stored materials leaned against the unit — these all choke airflow.

*(Instructor points to disconnect pull-out.)*

Now — the outdoor disconnect. It's the pull-out box or switch mounted on the wall near the condenser. Pull it out and set it aside.

*(Pulls disconnect — holds cartridge fuses in view.)*

Check those fuse cartridges if present. A blown fuse here will kill power to the condenser while the indoor unit still runs — which means you'd have a blower running and blowing warm air, and nothing happening outside.

Now open the electrical panel on the condenser itself.

*(Opens side panel.)*

You are now looking at live voltage when you close this panel back up with the disconnect in. High voltage is present at those input lugs — treat this panel with respect."

---

## SEGMENT 7 – Step 3 (Continued): Contactor Testing & Component Logic
**Time Range: 8:15 – 9:45** *(approx. 220 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor looks into open condenser panel, meter in hand.)*

"Inside this panel you'll see the contactor — it looks like a small relay block with heavy power lugs on top and smaller control wires on the bottom. The contactor is the gatekeeper. When the thermostat calls for cool, 24 volts gets sent out on that Y wire, energizes the contactor coil, and the contactor closes — connecting line voltage to the compressor and condenser fan motor.

First test — **is the contactor getting the call?**

Reinstall the disconnect so the system has power. Thermostat must be calling for cooling.

*(Reinstalls disconnect carefully.)*

With your meter on AC voltage, check the two small terminals on the contactor coil — that's 24 volts control voltage. If you read 24 volts and the contactor is NOT pulled in — it's not clicking and the plunger isn't down — you likely have a failed contactor. Replace it.

*(Shows meter probes at coil terminals — reading displayed.)*

If the contactor IS pulled in, now check line versus load voltage across the contactor.

Line side — the lugs at the top — should show full supply voltage, typically in the 208 to 240 volt range.

Load side — the lugs at the bottom — should show the same voltage if the contactor contacts are making good connection.

*(Meter probes move to load lugs.)*

If line is present and load is zero — the contactor contacts have failed. Replace the contactor.

If both line and load are present but the condenser fan motor isn't spinning, or you hear the compressor humming but not starting — that points to a failed capacitor, motor, or compressor. Those diagnostics are exactly what we cover in Weeks 2 and 3."

---

### Visual / Camera Notes:

- **Camera framing:** OTS ECU of instructor probing contactor terminals — meter face in frame. Side-angle MCU of contactor showing plunger up vs. down (pulled in).
- **B-roll:** ECU of contactor coil terminals with probes; ECU of meter displaying 24.1V at coil; ECU of contactor plunger visibly depressed (pulled in); ECU of line vs. load lugs.
- **UI overlays:**
  - Labeled diagram overlay of contactor: Coil terminals (24V), line lugs (L1/L2), load lugs (T1/T2) with arrows and voltage callouts.
  - Decision-tree graphic (3-second display):
    - "24V at coil + contactor NOT pulled in → Failed contactor"
    - "Contactor pulled in + load voltage = 0 → Failed contacts"
    - "Contactor pulled in + load voltage OK + motors not running → Capacitor/Motor/Compressor (see Weeks 2–3)"
- **Safety reminder inline (instructor dialogue):** "Reinstall the disconnect so the system has power" — brief overlay text: *"Stay clear of terminals when reinstalling disconnect."*
- **Transition:** Cut back to instructor indoors — standing at a workbench or table for the documentation segment.

## SEGMENT 8 – Step 4: Bucket the Problem & Document
**Time Range: 9:45 – 11:00** *(approx. 195 words)*

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at a clean workbench or table with a work order clipboard and tablet visible.)*

"Step four — and this step is what separates a good technician from a great one.

After you've run the diagnostic, you name what you found, and you put it in one of four buckets.

Bucket one: **Thermostat and control.** Setpoint issue, bad thermostat, broken control wire, failed control board, blown transformer fuse.

Bucket two: **Power.** Tripped breaker, blown fuse, failed contactor, failed transformer.

Bucket three: **Airflow.** Dirty filter, dirty coil, frozen coil, blocked return, condenser fin restriction.

Bucket four: **Refrigeration and components.** Failed capacitor, failed motor, failed compressor, low or overcharged refrigerant, refrigerant leak.

Now — here's what a real work order note looks like.

Example one:

*(Reads from clipboard.)*

'Unit found with thermostat set to Heat mode. Switched to Cool, setpoint adjusted to 72°F. System started and ran normally. No further issues at this time. Bucket: Thermostat/Control.'

Example two:

'Found outdoor disconnect fuses open. Replaced 30A cartridge fuses. System started, verified 240V at load side of contactor. Compressor and condenser fan running, airflow confirmed at supply. Bucket: Power.'

Clear, specific, and it tells the whole story. Not 'checked system, running ok.' That note helps no one — not the next tech, not your manager, not you at a callback."

---

### Suggested Quiz Questions (5 Questions, Directly Linked to Video Content)

**Question 1 — Multiple Choice**
*When you arrive at a no-cool call and the thermostat is set to Cool mode with the setpoint 2°F below room temperature, what should you do first?*
- A) Go directly to the outdoor condenser to check the contactor.
- B) Adjust the setpoint to at least 5°F below room temperature and observe the system response. ✓
- C) Pull the outdoor disconnect and check the fuses.
- D) Replace the air filter and retest.

---

**Question 2 — Multiple Choice**
*At the air handler's low-voltage terminal strip, you measure R-to-C and get 0 volts AC. The most likely causes include: (select the best answer)*
- A) Failed contactor or failed capacitor.
- B) Clogged condenser coil fins.
- C) Blown transformer fuse, tripped breaker, or failed transformer. ✓
- D) Frozen evaporator coil.

---

**Question 3 — Multiple Choice**
*You find the outdoor condenser not running. With the thermostat calling for cool, you measure 24V AC at the contactor coil terminals, but the contactor plunger is NOT pulled in. What does this indicate?*
- A) The thermostat is not sending a Y signal.
- B) The contactor coil has failed and the contactor should be replaced. ✓
- C) The compressor capacitor has failed.
- D) The indoor transformer has failed.

---

**Question 4 — Short Answer / Discussion**
*A technician finds the evaporator coil iced over. According to the four-step diagnostic pattern, what should the technician do immediately, and why should refrigerant NOT be checked at this point?*

*Expected answer elements: Switch thermostat to Fan Only to defrost the coil. Do not check refrigerant while the coil is frozen because ice blocks access to the coil surface and gives false readings — root cause must be determined (airflow or refrigerant) after defrost. Fix any airflow restrictions first.*

---

**Question 5 — Scenario / Multiple Choice**
*After completing a no-cool call, the technician's work order note reads: "Checked system — running OK." Which of the following is the BEST revision of this work order note?*
- A) "AC unit fixed. Resident satisfied."
- B) "Outdoor unit was not running. Replaced blown 30A fuses in disconnect. Verified 240V at load side of contactor. Compressor and condenser fan running. Bucket: Power." ✓
- C) "Outdoor unit inspected. Fuses replaced. System OK."
- D) "No-cool call. Found and repaired electrical issue. System operational."
