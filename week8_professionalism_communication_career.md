## Learning Objectives

By the end of this video, a technician will be able to:
1. Apply the communication framework for resident interaction on HVAC service calls — from entry to exit.
2. Describe the requirements for EPA Section 608 certification and identify the correct certification type for their role.
3. Identify at least two continuing education pathways (NATE, EPA 608, manufacturer training) and state the career value of each.

## SEGMENT 2 – The Service Call Communication Framework
**Time Range: 1:00 – 3:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at apartment door — knocking, resident answers.)*

"Every service call is two interactions: one with the equipment and one with the resident. The equipment doesn't care how you communicate. The resident does.

Here's the framework I use on every call, regardless of how technical the issue is.

**At entry:** Identify yourself clearly. 'Hi, I'm [name] from maintenance — I'm here about the AC.' Wear your ID badge. If you're new to the resident, they may not know you. Give them a moment to confirm you're who you say you are before walking in.

*(Instructor steps inside — gestures around.)*

**During the call:** Residents want to know what you're doing and why. You don't need to give a lecture, but a brief, plain-language update makes a huge difference.

'I'm going to start at the thermostat, then check the air handler, and then go outside to the condenser. Should take about 15 to 20 minutes.'

That one sentence tells the resident: you have a plan, you know what you're doing, and they're not going to be wondering what's happening for the next half hour.

If you find something and need to leave to get a part — tell them. Don't just walk out.

'I found a failed capacitor on your outdoor unit. I need to grab a replacement from the shop. I'll be back within the hour and we'll have your AC running today.'

**At exit:** Always close the loop before you leave. Tell the resident the current status — even if the system isn't fixed yet.

'Your system is running now. The filter was severely clogged — I replaced it and the system came back on. I'd recommend checking that filter every 30 days. If you notice it's not cooling by this evening, please call us back.'

Plain language. Status update. Next step. That's the exit framework."

## SEGMENT 3 – Managing Difficult Interactions
**Time Range: 3:00 – 4:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor — direct, composed tone.)*

"You're going to get frustrated residents. Someone who's been without AC for two days, or who's been promised a repair three times, or who just had a bad day. Here's how you handle it.

**Acknowledge first.** Before you explain anything, acknowledge the situation. 'I understand — being without AC in this heat is miserable, and I appreciate your patience.' Two sentences. It doesn't commit you to anything, but it signals that you're on their side.

**Separate what you know from what you don't.** If you don't know the cause yet, say that — clearly. 'I need to run through my diagnostic before I can tell you exactly what the issue is. I'll give you a full update as soon as I know.'

Overpromising is a trust-killer. Saying 'it'll be fixed in an hour' and then finding a failed compressor is far worse than saying 'I'm going to check it thoroughly and give you an honest update.'

**What's not your call — say so.** If a resident asks 'when is this getting replaced?' and you don't know — that's not your decision to make. 'That's a decision that goes to management. What I can tell you is I'm documenting everything I find today so they have the full picture.'

That last phrase — 'I'm documenting everything' — is powerful. It tells the resident their situation is being taken seriously and recorded, not ignored.

You cannot control the equipment. You can control how you show up. Residents remember how they were treated far longer than they remember the repair."

## SEGMENT 4 – EPA Section 608 Certification
**Time Range: 4:30 – 6:00**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at desk — certification card and study materials visible.)*

"If you're going to work on refrigerant systems beyond what we've covered in visual inspections and electrical diagnosis, you need EPA Section 608 certification. This is a federal requirement under the Clean Air Act.

Section 608 has four certification types:

**Type I** — Small appliances. Systems with 5 pounds or less of refrigerant — window units, refrigerators.
**Type II** — High-pressure systems. This covers the R-410A and R-22 split systems you're working on in multifamily.
**Type III** — Low-pressure systems. Chillers and larger commercial equipment using R-123, R-11.
**Universal** — Covers all three types. This is the one to pursue if you plan to build a long career in HVAC.

What does 608 allow you to do? Purchase refrigerants above 2 pounds, recover refrigerant instead of venting it, and perform refrigerant work legally.

The exam is administered by an EPA-approved certification organization — ESCO Group, North American Technician Excellence, and others. It's a written exam — typically 25 questions per type — and you can take it at approved testing sites or proctored online.

Study materials: the course materials for this series include a 608 Type II study guide. For Universal, the full ARI/ASHRAE study manual is your best resource.

*(Holds up certification card.)*

Get your 608. It's a one-time investment that opens every door in this field."

## SEGMENT 5 – Continuing Education Pathways
**Time Range: 6:00 – 7:30**

---

### Voiceover / On-Camera Script:

*(Instructor at a board or display showing a career pathway graphic.)*

"EPA 608 is the floor, not the ceiling. Here's where you go from here.

**NATE — North American Technician Excellence.** This is the most widely recognized certification in the HVAC industry. NATE certifications are available in specific areas: air conditioning, heat pumps, gas heating, light commercial. They require work experience and a proctored exam. A NATE-certified technician commands higher pay and more responsibility.

**Manufacturer training.** Every major brand — Carrier, Lennox, Trane, Daikin, York — offers training programs, often free or low-cost for technicians who work on their equipment. These programs cover brand-specific controls, communicating systems, and advanced diagnostics. They're also a direct pipeline to the manufacturer's technical support line — which is invaluable on a complex call.

**R-32 and A2L refrigerant training.** The industry is transitioning to lower-GWP refrigerants — R-32, R-454B, and others classified as A2L — mildly flammable. New equipment using these refrigerants is entering multifamily now. A2L safety training will be a requirement for working on this equipment, and early certification puts you ahead of the curve.

**State and local licenses.** Many states require HVAC contractors to be licensed, and some require individual technician licensing. Know your state's requirements. Your employer should support you in meeting them.

The path forward is clear. EPA 608 → NATE → Manufacturer training → Ongoing. Every certification you earn is evidence of your skill and your commitment."
